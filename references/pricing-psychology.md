# Pricing Psychology in E-Commerce: Research Findings

**Research Date**: March 9, 2026
**Total Findings**: 20
**Methodology**: Web-based literature review of academic papers, practitioner case studies, and A/B test reports

---

## Summary

### Top 3 Most Impactful Findings

1. **Finding 3 (Charm Pricing Field Test)**: Anderson & Simester demonstrated that a $39 price outsold both $34 and $44 for the same dress in a mail-order catalog, proving that 9-ending prices can increase demand even at higher absolute prices.
2. **Finding 6 (Decoy Effect - Ariely's Economist Experiment)**: Adding a dominated decoy option shifted preference from 32% to 84% for the target option -- a 163% relative increase in choice share with zero change to the actual offerings.
3. **Finding 15 (Free Shipping Threshold - NuFace)**: Adding a "$75 free shipping" threshold increased orders by 90% and AOV by 7.32%, demonstrating that free shipping thresholds are one of the highest-leverage conversion tools available.

## Findings

### Finding 1: Anchoring Effects on Price Judgments — Foundational Evidence
- **Source**: Tversky, A. & Kahneman, D. (1974). "Judgment Under Uncertainty: Heuristics and Biases." *Science*, 185(4157), 1124-1131. Also: Ariely, D., Loewenstein, G., & Prelec, D. (2003). "Coherent Arbitrariness: Stable Demand Curves Without Stable Preferences." *Quarterly Journal of Economics*, 118(1), 73-106.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold
- **Methodology**: Tversky & Kahneman: participants estimated quantities after exposure to random anchor numbers (wheel of fortune). Ariely et al.: MIT MBA students used last two digits of SSN as anchor before bidding on consumer goods.
- **Key Finding**: Arbitrary numerical anchors systematically bias subsequent value judgments. In Ariely's study, participants with high SSN digits (80-99) bid 216-346% more than those with low digits (01-20) for identical products. The anchor effect persists even when the anchor is transparently irrelevant.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Display higher-priced options first (e.g., premium tier before basic tier). Show original MSRP before sale price. Use "compare at" pricing to set an anchor. The first number a shopper sees becomes the reference point for value assessment.
- **Replication Status**: Extensively replicated. Anchoring is one of the most robust findings in behavioral economics, confirmed across hundreds of studies and meta-analyses (Furnham & Boo, 2011).
- **Boundary Conditions**: Effect is reduced (but not eliminated) when participants have domain expertise. Less effective when consumers have strong prior price knowledge for the specific product. Extreme anchors can trigger suspicion.

### Finding 2: Multi-Unit Pricing Framing Increases Purchase Quantity
- **Source**: Manning, K.C. & Sprott, D.E. (2007). "Multiple Unit Price Promotions and Their Effects on Quantity Purchase Intentions." *Journal of Retailing*, 83(4), 411-421. Also supported by broader anchoring literature (Tversky & Kahneman, 1974).
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold
- **Methodology**: Multiple experimental studies testing multi-unit pricing formats ("3 for $5") vs. per-unit pricing ("$1.67 each") on purchase quantity intentions.
- **Key Finding**: Multi-unit pricing (e.g., "3 for $5") increases purchase quantities compared to equivalent per-unit pricing. The number in the multi-unit offer serves as a quantity anchor. Effect is consistent with broader anchoring research.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Present bundle quantities with a single combined price rather than per-unit pricing. "3 for $29.99" will outperform "$9.99 each" even when the math is identical. The number in the multi-unit offer serves as an implicit purchase quantity anchor.
- **Replication Status**: Supported by multiple studies on quantity anchoring. Consistent with broader anchoring literature.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Works best for consumable/replenishable goods. Less effective for unique or high-consideration purchases.
- **NOTE**: Earlier versions of this finding cited Wansink (1998) with specific lift percentages (112%, 32%). Wansink's work has been discredited — 18 papers retracted, resigned from Cornell in 2018 for data fabrication. The underlying anchoring principle is well-supported by independent research (Tversky & Kahneman, Ariely, Manning & Sprott), but Wansink's specific numbers should not be cited.

### Finding 3: Charm Pricing ($39 vs. $34) Increases Demand in Mail-Order Catalog
- **Source**: Anderson, E.T. & Simester, D.I. (2003). "Effects of $9 Price Endings on Retail Sales: Evidence from Field Experiments." *Quantitative Marketing and Economics*, 1(1), 93-110. (MIT Sloan / University of Chicago)
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold
- **Methodology**: Controlled field experiment with a national mail-order catalog. Same women's clothing item was randomly assigned prices of $34, $39, or $44 across different catalog versions sent to comparable customer groups.
- **Key Finding**: The $39 price sold 21 units; $34 sold 16 units; $44 sold 17 units. The 9-ending price at $39 outsold the lower price at $34 by approximately 31%. This is not just a left-digit effect -- $39 is absolutely higher than $34, yet it generated more demand.
- **E-Commerce Application**: When setting prices, test 9-ending variants even if they are higher than the current price. The signaling effect of a 9-ending ("this is a deal") can outweigh the higher absolute cost.
- **Replication Status**: Replicated across multiple product categories within the same paper. Consistent with the broader charm pricing literature.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Effect was strongest for new items and items not previously purchased by the customer. Diminished for repeat purchases where customers had a reference price. Does NOT work for luxury/prestige goods (see Finding 7).

### Finding 4: Left-Digit Effect Mechanism in Price Perception
- **Source**: Thomas, M. & Morwitz, V. (2005). "Penny Wise and Pound Foolish: The Left-Digit Effect in Price Cognition." *Journal of Consumer Research*, 32(1), 54-64.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold
- **Methodology**: Five controlled experiments testing how consumers encode multi-digit prices, comparing price pairs where left digits differ ($2.99 vs. $3.00) vs. where they don't ($3.59 vs. $3.60).
- **Key Finding**: The left-digit effect occurs *only* when the leftmost digits differ (e.g., $2.99 vs. $3.00). The encoded magnitude of $2.99 is anchored on "2" and perceived as significantly less than $3.00, but $3.59 vs. $3.60 shows no comparable effect. The closer two competing prices are numerically, the stronger the left-digit effect becomes.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Charm pricing is most powerful when it changes the left digit ($3.00 to $2.99, $50 to $49.99). A price of $3.79 vs. $3.80 will show minimal effect because the left digit doesn't change. Prioritize left-digit reduction over simply ending in .99.
- **Replication Status**: Replicated. One of the most-cited papers in pricing psychology.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Effect is moderated by involvement level and numerical processing style. Consumers who engage in deliberate calculation are less susceptible. Effect diminishes when prices are presented side-by-side (easier comparison).

### Finding 5: Decoy Effect Replication Challenges
- **Source**: Comprehensive meta-review cited in multiple sources (Frederick, S., Lee, L., & Baskin, E., 2014, and subsequent reviews). Also: Wikipedia's synthesis of Huber, Payne, & Puto (1982) original and subsequent replication attempts.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold
- **Methodology**: Review of 91 replication attempts of the attraction/decoy effect across 23 product categories.
- **Key Finding**: Of 91 attempts to reproduce the attraction effect, only 11 produced reliable results. The effect is real but far more context-dependent than popularly presented. It works most consistently in controlled lab settings with simple choice sets.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Do not assume the decoy effect will automatically work in your pricing page. Test rigorously. The effect is strongest when: (a) options are easy to compare on 2 clear dimensions, (b) the decoy is clearly dominated on all dimensions, (c) choice sets are small (3 options).
- **Replication Status**: Partially replicated. Robust in specific conditions, unreliable in others.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Fails when consumers have strong prior preferences, when choice sets are complex (>4 options), or when the decoy is not clearly dominated. Real-world noise reduces effect size compared to lab settings.

### Finding 6: Ariely's Economist Decoy Experiment
- **Source**: Ariely, D. (2008). *Predictably Irrational*. Chapter 1. Experiment conducted at MIT with student participants.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold
- **Quality Flag**: Classroom demonstration, not formal peer-reviewed experiment; directional only
- **Methodology**: 100 MIT students chose between Economist subscription options. Condition A: Web-only ($59), Print-only ($125), Print+Web ($125). Condition B: Web-only ($59), Print+Web ($125). The print-only option at $125 serves as the decoy (dominated by print+web at the same price).
- **Key Finding**: With decoy present: 16% chose web-only, 0% chose print-only, 84% chose print+web. Without decoy: 68% chose web-only, 32% chose print+web. The decoy shifted preference for the target option from 32% to 84% -- a 163% relative increase.
- **E-Commerce Application**: When designing pricing tiers (especially SaaS/subscription), include a "decoy" tier that is clearly dominated by the target tier. The decoy should match the target on price but offer strictly less value. This makes the target option feel like an obvious bargain.
- **Replication Status**: Classroom demonstration, not a formal peer-reviewed experiment. Directionally consistent with Huber et al. (1982) lab findings. See Finding 5 for replication caveats.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Student sample, hypothetical choice (no real purchase). Effect size in real purchasing contexts is likely smaller. The decoy must be obviously dominated -- subtle differences may not trigger the effect.

### Finding 7: Charm Pricing Backfires for Luxury and Premium Brands
- **Source**: Ortega & Tabares (2023), cited in newneuromarketing.com review of when charm pricing backfires. Also consistent with Stiving & Winer (1997) and broader prestige pricing literature.
- **Evidence Tier**: Bronze
- **Methodology**: Consumer perception studies comparing round vs. 99-ending prices for luxury and premium brand products.
- **Key Finding**: For brands associated with high quality or luxury (e.g., Apple, premium fashion), charm pricing (e.g., $999.99 vs. $1,000) reduces perceived quality. Consumers' brains treat charm pricing as a "cheap trick." Luxury brands that use 99-endings may inadvertently cheapen their brand image.
- **E-Commerce Application**: If selling premium or luxury products, use round prices ($200, $1,000, $50). Reserve charm pricing for value-oriented products, everyday purchases, and impulse buys. The decision between charm and round pricing should be driven by brand positioning, not applied uniformly.
- **Replication Status**: Consistent across multiple studies in the prestige pricing literature.
- **Boundary Conditions**: This is specifically about brand-quality perception. For unknown/new brands without established premium positioning, charm pricing may still increase conversion. The boundary is brand perception, not product price level.

### Finding 8: Loss Aversion Ratio (Prospect Theory Foundation)
- **Source**: Kahneman, D. & Tversky, A. (1979). "Prospect Theory: An Analysis of Decision under Risk." *Econometrica*, 47(2), 263-292. Refined in Tversky & Kahneman (1992).
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold
- **Methodology**: Series of choice experiments presenting participants with gambles involving gains and losses of varying magnitudes. The 1992 paper estimated the loss aversion coefficient empirically.
- **Key Finding**: Losses are felt approximately 1.5x to 2.5x more intensely than equivalent gains. The commonly cited ratio is approximately 2:1 (often referenced as 2.25:1 from the 1992 paper). This means a $10 loss creates roughly the same emotional magnitude as a $20-$25 gain.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Frame promotions as "avoiding a loss" rather than "gaining a benefit." "Don't miss out on $20 savings" is psychologically stronger than "Save $20." Countdown timers, expiring discounts, and "last chance" messaging leverage this asymmetry directly.
- **Replication Status**: Replicated extensively. One of the most robust findings in behavioral economics, though exact ratio varies by context (1.5x to 2.5x range).
- **Boundary Conditions**: Loss aversion diminishes for very small amounts (trivial losses don't trigger the effect) and in domains where the person has significant expertise/experience. Some recent papers have questioned the universality of the 2:1 ratio.

### Finding 9: Countdown Timers and Scarcity Framing Conversion Lifts
- **Source**: Multiple practitioner A/B tests. Obvi (DTC supplement brand) A/B test; Bose 24-hour sale landing page test. Compiled by various CRO platforms.
- **Evidence Tier**: Bronze
- **Methodology**: Obvi: A/B test on bundle promotion page with vs. without countdown timer. Bose: 24-hour flash sale landing page with countdown timer vs. control.
- **Key Finding**: Obvi saw a 7-8% increase in conversions and 13.7% boost in revenue per user with the countdown timer variant. Bose saw a 34% increase in click-through rate and 22% lift in conversions during their countdown-timer-backed 24-hour sale.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Add countdown timers to time-limited promotions, flash sales, and cart pages. Combine with specific end times ("Offer ends in 2h 14m") rather than vague urgency ("Limited time!"). The timer creates a concrete loss frame -- the deal disappearing.
- **Replication Status**: Consistent across multiple practitioner tests. Exact effect sizes vary widely (7-34% range in these examples). No peer-reviewed RCT.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Overuse causes "urgency fatigue" -- if every page has a countdown timer, customers learn to ignore them. Fake urgency (timers that reset) destroys trust. Most effective for genuinely time-limited offers.

### Finding 10: Mere Touch Increases Perceived Ownership and Valuation
- **Source**: Peck, J. & Shu, S.B. (2009). "The Effect of Mere Touch on Perceived Ownership." *Journal of Consumer Research*, 36(3), 434-447.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold
- **Methodology**: Multiple lab experiments testing whether physical contact with an object increases perceived ownership and willingness-to-pay. Controlled for prior interest and product type.
- **Key Finding**: Merely touching an object significantly increases perceived ownership and, consequently, the amount participants were willing to pay for it. The effect is mediated by perceived ownership -- touch creates a sense of "this is mine" that increases valuation.
- **E-Commerce Application**: For online stores where physical touch is impossible, use "ownership imagery" -- language and visuals that encourage customers to imagine the product in their possession ("Imagine this on your desk," "Picture yourself wearing this"). Product customization tools, AR try-on features, and 360-degree views serve as touch surrogates.
- **Replication Status**: Replicated. Extended by subsequent research on haptic imagery and touchscreen interfaces.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Effect is stronger for individuals with high "need for touch." Online context inherently lacks physical touch, so surrogates (imagery, interactivity) have smaller effect sizes than actual touch.

### Finding 11: Endowment Effect -- WTA/WTP Ratio of 2:1
- **Source**: Kahneman, D., Knetsch, J.L., & Thaler, R.H. (1990). "Experimental Tests of the Endowment Effect and the Coase Theorem." *Journal of Political Economy*, 98(6), 1325-1348.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold
- **Methodology**: Cornell University undergraduates randomly assigned as "sellers" (given a coffee mug) or "buyers" (not given a mug). Willingness-to-accept (WTA) and willingness-to-pay (WTP) were elicited in market-like conditions with real transactions.
- **Key Finding**: Median selling prices (WTA) were approximately 2x median buying prices (WTP) for identical mugs. Sellers demanded roughly twice what buyers were willing to pay, purely as a function of ownership status. The effect persisted across multiple rounds, showing no learning/extinction.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Once a customer adds an item to their cart, they begin to psychologically "own" it. Cart abandonment emails should frame the items as something the customer *already has* that they're about to lose ("Your items are waiting" / "Don't lose what you picked out"), leveraging the endowment effect + loss aversion. Wishlists, saved carts, and "reserved for you" messaging all amplify this.
- **Replication Status**: Replicated extensively. One of the foundational findings in behavioral economics.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Effect is reduced when participants are experienced traders, when goods are purchased for resale (not personal use), and for goods the person doesn't value. Some recent meta-analyses suggest the ratio may be closer to 1.5:1 in real-world markets.

### Finding 12: Rule of 100 -- Percentage vs. Dollar Discount Framing
- **Source**: Berger, J. (2013). *Contagious: Why Things Catch On*. Simon & Schuster. Originally presented in Berger's marketing research and teaching.
- **Evidence Tier**: Bronze
- **Quality Flag**: Published book by Wharton professor; conceptual framework without controlled experiment
- **Methodology**: Conceptual framework based on perceived magnitude of discounts, supported by consumer behavior observations and classroom demonstrations.
- **Key Finding**: For products priced under $100, percentage discounts appear larger (20% off a $50 item = $10, but "20% off" sounds bigger than "$10 off"). For products over $100, absolute dollar discounts appear larger ($500 off a $2,000 laptop sounds bigger than "25% off," even though they're identical). The $100 threshold is the crossover point.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Dynamically display discount format based on product price. Under $100: show "Save 25%." Over $100: show "Save $50." This simple conditional formatting maximizes the perceived size of every discount without changing the actual discount amount.
- **Replication Status**: Not formally tested in a controlled experiment as published. Widely adopted as a practitioner heuristic. Directionally consistent with numerical cognition research.
- **Boundary Conditions**: The $100 threshold is a heuristic, not a precise crossover point. The actual crossover depends on the specific discount percentage and absolute amount. For amounts near $100, test both formats.

### Finding 13: Dollar-Off Coupons Can Generate 170% More Revenue Than Percentage-Off
- **Source**: MarketingSherpa case study. A/B test comparing coupon types for an unnamed retailer.
- **Evidence Tier**: Silver
- **Methodology**: A/B test comparing a $50-off coupon vs. a 15%-off coupon (not equivalent discounts -- the $50 off was more generous for most order values).
- **Key Finding**: The $50-off coupon generated 170% more revenue and had a 72% higher conversion rate than the 15%-off coupon. Note: these are not equivalent discounts, so the result conflates discount generosity with framing format.
- **E-Commerce Application**: When offering coupons, the perceived concreteness of a dollar amount ("$50 off") may feel more tangible and actionable than a percentage ("15% off"), especially when the dollar amount is generous. However, always ensure you're comparing equivalent discount values when testing format.
- **Replication Status**: Single case study. The confound (non-equivalent discount amounts) limits generalizability of the framing effect specifically.
- **Boundary Conditions**: This specific result is confounded by different discount magnitudes. The 170% figure should NOT be cited as evidence that dollar-off framing is inherently superior -- only that $50 off outperformed 15% off in this specific context (which is unsurprising given the different actual values).

### Finding 14: Bundle Pricing Increases Revenue 10-30% and Perceived Value 20-25%
- **Source**: McKinsey & Company (bundling strategy research, cited in multiple practitioner reviews). Deloitte (personalized bundling study). Harvard Business Review (mixed vs. pure bundling comparison).
- **Evidence Tier**: Silver
- **Methodology**: Cross-industry analysis of bundling strategies and their revenue impact. Specific methodologies vary by source.
- **Key Finding**: McKinsey: effective bundling strategies increase revenues by 10-30%. Deloitte: personalized bundling yields up to 40% higher retention and 22% higher average transaction values. HBR: mixed bundling (buy individually or as bundle) outperforms pure bundling (bundle only) by 25-35% in revenue generation. Well-designed bundles boost perceived value by 20-25%.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Offer bundles as an option alongside individual products (mixed bundling). Show the individual prices summed up next to the bundle price to make savings salient. Personalize bundle recommendations based on browsing/purchase history for maximum impact.
- **Replication Status**: Industry data, not controlled experiments. Directional findings are consistent across multiple consulting reports.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Bundling can reduce revenue if the products bundled have very different willingness-to-pay distributions (forcing a single price on heterogeneous valuations). Too many bundle options create choice overload (can reduce conversion by up to 10%).

### Finding 15: Free Shipping Threshold Increases Orders 90% (NuFace Case Study)
- **Source**: Red Door Interactive / VWO. NuFace A/B test case study.
- **Evidence Tier**: Bronze
- **Methodology**: A/B test on NuFace (anti-aging skincare) e-commerce site. Control: original site. Variant: "Free shipping over $75!" messaging added prominently above the Shop button. Traffic split 50/50.
- **Key Finding**: Orders increased by 90% (96% statistical confidence). Average order value increased by 7.32%. The $75 threshold was set above the existing AOV to encourage basket-building behavior.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Set free shipping thresholds 15-25% above current AOV. Place the messaging prominently and early in the shopping flow (global header, product pages) -- not buried in checkout. Use dynamic progress bars showing how close the customer is to qualifying.
- **Replication Status**: Single case study, but directionally consistent with broad e-commerce shipping research. Specific 90% lift is unusually high and may reflect the fact that NuFace had previously hidden their free shipping offer.
- **Boundary Conditions**: The 90% lift was partly because free shipping was already offered but not visible -- the test was as much about *communicating* the offer as *adding* it. Results will be smaller for sites that already prominently display shipping information. Threshold set too high creates friction and reduces conversion.

### Finding 16: Free Shipping Threshold Optimization Data
- **Source**: Multiple industry sources: FedEx consumer survey data (2025), Cartly Pro analysis, Intelligems merchant data.
- **Evidence Tier**: Bronze
- **Methodology**: Survey data and cross-merchant analysis of shipping threshold performance.
- **Key Finding**: 58% of shoppers add items to their cart specifically to reach a free shipping threshold. 48% of shoppers who planned to "think about it" convert immediately when free shipping is available. Dynamic progress indicators ("You're $12 away from free shipping!") increase threshold conversion by 15-25% compared to static messaging. Most e-commerce thresholds fall between $35-$100, with ~$64 as the industry average. Best implementations see 15-30% AOV increases with only 5-10% conversion decreases, yielding 5-15% net revenue growth.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Implement a dynamic free shipping progress bar in the cart. Link it to recommended add-on products that would push the order over the threshold. A/B test the threshold amount -- too high reduces conversion; too low leaves AOV gains on the table.
- **Replication Status**: Consistent across multiple merchant datasets and surveys.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Effectiveness varies by product category and baseline AOV. Categories with low-cost add-ons (accessories, consumables) benefit most. Threshold is less effective for single-SKU stores or very high-ticket items.

### Finding 17: "Pennies-a-Day" Temporal Reframing Increases Compliance
- **Source**: Gourville, J.T. (1998). "Pennies-a-Day: The Effect of Temporal Reframing on Transaction Evaluation." *Journal of Consumer Research*, 24(4), 395-408.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold
- **Methodology**: Series of laboratory studies testing how reframing an aggregate cost into a smaller per-day/per-week equivalent changes purchase likelihood. Tested across multiple product categories.
- **Key Finding**: Reframing a large one-time cost as a small daily cost ("less than $1/day" instead of "$350/year") systematically increases transaction compliance. The mechanism: daily framing causes consumers to retrieve small everyday expenses (a cup of coffee) as the comparison set, making the product seem trivially cheap. Aggregate framing triggers comparison to large purchases (a TV, a vacation), making the same product seem expensive.
- **E-Commerce Application**: For subscriptions and high-ticket items, always show the per-day or per-month equivalent alongside the total price. "$2.49/day" feels trivial even though it's $75/month or $900/year. SaaS pricing pages that show "per day" equivalents leverage this directly.
- **Replication Status**: Replicated across product categories within the original paper. Consistent with mental accounting theory (Thaler, 1985).
- **Boundary Conditions**: Most effective when the per-day amount can be compared to a trivially small daily expense. Less effective when the per-day amount is still high (e.g., "$15/day" doesn't feel trivial). The actual payment structure must still be clearly disclosed -- deceptive framing erodes trust.

### Finding 18: Subscription Commitment Anxiety and 70% Forgotten Subscriptions
- **Source**: ResearchGate publication: "Unsubscribe From Anxiety: The Psychological Costs of Subscription Service Overload" (2024). Multiple consumer surveys on subscription fatigue.
- **Evidence Tier**: Bronze
- **Methodology**: Consumer survey and behavioral analysis of subscription management patterns.
- **Key Finding**: Up to 70% of consumers report subscribing to services they forget about or rarely use. Subscription overload creates anxiety, guilt, exhaustion, and financial strain. Key psychological mechanisms keeping subscribers locked in: loss aversion (reluctance to lose access), status quo bias (preference for maintaining existing arrangements), sunk-cost effect (continuing because of past payments), and endowment effect (sense of ownership over the service).
- **E-Commerce Application**: For subscription businesses: reduce commitment anxiety by offering easy cancellation, pause options, and flexible plans. Paradoxically, lowering the perceived barrier to exit increases initial sign-up rates. "Cancel anytime" messaging leverages this. For one-time purchase businesses competing against subscriptions: emphasize "no commitment, no recurring charges" as a differentiator.
- **Replication Status**: Survey-based findings consistent across multiple consumer research reports.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Subscription fatigue is primarily a concern in markets with many competing subscriptions (media, SaaS, meal kits). For unique/essential services with few alternatives, commitment anxiety is lower.

### Finding 19: Price-Quality Inference -- Higher Prices Increase Quality Expectations but Not Post-Experience Ratings
- **Source**: Kurz, P. et al. (2023). "Pricey therefore good? Price affects expectations, but not quality perceptions and liking." *Psychology & Marketing* (Wiley). Also: Scitovszky (1944-45) foundational work on price-quality heuristics.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold
- **Methodology**: Experimental studies measuring both pre-consumption expectations and post-consumption quality ratings for products at different price points.
- **Key Finding**: Higher prices led to higher quality and liking *expectations* as predicted by the price-quality heuristic. However, after actually experiencing the product, there was no consistent effect of price on perceived quality or liking. The price-quality inference operates primarily at the pre-purchase evaluation stage.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Higher prices can increase *pre-purchase* conversion for products that cannot be easily evaluated before buying (experience goods, new products, unfamiliar brands). But post-purchase satisfaction depends on actual quality. Pricing above market for a mediocre product will increase returns and negative reviews. Price-quality inference is most useful for products where quality is genuinely ambiguous before purchase.
- **Replication Status**: The specific finding (expectations yes, experience no) is from a 2023 study. The broader price-quality heuristic has mixed replication results -- it's stronger in some categories than others.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Price-quality inference is strongest when: (a) consumers have limited product knowledge, (b) there is high variance in actual quality across brands, (c) other quality cues (reviews, brand reputation) are absent. It is weakest for familiar categories where consumers have strong prior preferences.

### Finding 20: Price-Quality Inference Increases With Brand Differentiation
- **Source**: Verma, D.P.S. & Sen Gupta, S. (2004). "Does Higher Price Signal Better Quality?" *Vikalpa: The Journal for Decision Makers*, 29(2). Also: Simon-Kucher & Partners (consulting research on price-quality relationships).
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold
- **Methodology**: Consumer perception studies examining when higher prices successfully signal quality across different product categories and brand environments.
- **Key Finding**: When consumers perceive greater variance among brands, their reliance on price as a quality cue increases. In categories where brands are perceived as interchangeable, price has less influence on quality perception. Marketers who invest in brand differentiation amplify the effectiveness of premium pricing.
- **E-Commerce Application**: If selling premium products, invest in differentiating your brand (unique design, exclusive materials, superior customer service) before raising prices. Price increases without differentiation will not trigger the price-quality inference effectively. Show comparison tables, unique feature callouts, and quality certifications alongside premium prices.
- **Replication Status**: Consistent across the price-quality literature.
- **Boundary Conditions**: In commodity categories with low perceived brand variance, higher prices simply reduce demand without quality inference benefits. The effect also weakens when abundant reviews and ratings provide direct quality signals, reducing reliance on price as a proxy.

## Methodological Notes and Caveats

1. **Lab vs. Field**: Many of the foundational studies (Ariely's decoy, Kahneman's endowment, Thomas & Morwitz's left-digit) were conducted in laboratory settings. Real-world effect sizes are typically smaller due to noise, competing cues, and consumer heterogeneity.

2. **Publication Bias**: The pricing psychology literature has a significant publication bias toward positive findings. The decoy effect replication data (Finding 5: only 11/91 reliable replications) is a cautionary example.

3. **Practitioner Case Studies**: Findings from VWO, MarketingSherpa, and similar sources (Findings 9, 13, 15) are not peer-reviewed. Sample sizes, test durations, and confounds are often not fully disclosed. Treat specific numbers as directional rather than definitive.

4. **Context Dependence**: Nearly every finding has boundary conditions. Charm pricing backfires for luxury brands. Decoy effects fail with complex choice sets. Loss aversion framing loses power with overuse. Always test in your specific context.

5. **Insufficient Data Found**: For the endowment effect specifically in digital cart/wishlist contexts (Question 5), no controlled experiment measuring the effect of cart-adding on purchase probability was found. The application is inferred from the broader endowment effect literature (Findings 10, 11) rather than directly measured in an e-commerce cart setting.
